The Problem

Australia has no central register of where philanthropic money goes. Grants data is scattered across hundreds of government portals, foundation websites, and annual reports. Small organisations — especially those working with First Nations communities — spend weeks searching for the right grants, often competing for the same oversubscribed pools while other funding sits underused.

The information asymmetry is stark: 24 major foundations control 62% of all tracked philanthropy ($1.3B). Most community organisations don't know this, and can't see the full picture of what's available.

Collect from public sources

We pull grants data from government portals (GrantConnect, ARC, state programs) and foundation annual reports (via ACNC). Every source is open and free.

2

Enrich with AI

Raw data often has just a name and a dollar figure. We use AI to scrape programme websites, extract descriptions, categorise by sector, and identify eligibility criteria — turning sparse records into something you can actually search.

3

Build semantic embeddings

Every grant and foundation programme gets a mathematical fingerprint (an "embedding") that captures what it's about, not just the keywords. This lets you search by describing your needs in plain language, not by guessing the right search term.

4

Simulate and analyse

The Monte Carlo engine uses real win-rate data by source type, log-normal grant size distributions, and your organisation's parameters to simulate thousands of possible years — giving you a realistic picture of grant revenue potential, not just a best-case fantasy.

Why This Matters

Australia gives $13 billion a year to charity. But the organisations closest to communities — the small ones, the regional ones, the culturally led ones — often can't find the funding that's meant for them.

This tool makes the invisible visible. When you can see the full map of where money flows, you can plan a smarter path to sustainability — not just chase the same grants as everyone else.

A Note on the Simulation

The Monte Carlo simulator is a planning tool, not a prediction. It uses historical win rates by funding source type (local government: ~35%, small foundations: ~25%, federal programs: ~8%), applies your alignment score as a modifier, and samples grant sizes from a log-normal distribution calibrated to real averages.

Running 10,000 simulations gives you a distribution of outcomes — from the bottom 10% (tough year) to the top 10% (great year). The median is your most realistic planning number. The spread tells you how much uncertainty your current mix carries.

More diverse funding sources = narrower spread = more predictable revenue. That's the single most useful insight the simulator reveals.
