# Community-Led Funding Models in Australia: Beyond Grants and Philanthropy

Australia's social impact sector is overwhelmingly dependent on two funding sources: government grants and philanthropic foundations. An estimated 80% of community organisations rely on short-term grant cycles, creating a perpetual state of precarity that undermines long-term planning and community ownership.

## The Problem with Grant Dependency

The grant model concentrates power in the hands of funders. Organisations must shape their work to match funder priorities rather than community needs. Reporting requirements consume staff time, and the competitive application process pits organisations against each other instead of fostering collaboration. When grants end, programs disappear — regardless of community demand.

In Australia, the top 25 Private Ancillary Funds (PAFs) control over $8 billion in assets. These vehicles offer significant tax advantages to wealthy donors while giving communities little say in how funds are directed. The Paul Ramsay Foundation alone holds approximately $4 billion, making it larger than many government departments.

## Emerging Alternatives

Several community-led funding models are gaining traction across Australia:

**Community Energy**: CORENA (Citizens Own Renewable Energy Network Australia) operates a revolving fund model where communities fund solar installations, and the energy savings repay the fund for future projects. Since 2013, CORENA has funded over 30 projects without any government grants or corporate sponsorship — funded entirely by citizen donations.

**Cooperatives**: Australia has over 1,700 cooperatives with combined annual revenue exceeding $35 billion. The cooperative model — member-owned, democratically governed — offers a proven structure for community self-determination. Examples include community-owned grocery stores in remote areas and housing cooperatives providing affordable accommodation.

**Timebanking**: Networks like Timebanking Australia enable communities to exchange services using time as currency. One hour of work earns one time credit, regardless of the service — equalising the value of all labour and building social capital outside the cash economy.

**Community Development Finance Institutions (CDFIs)**: While Australia lags behind the UK and US in this space, organisations like Many Rivers Microfinance provide small loans to people excluded from mainstream banking, particularly First Nations communities and new migrants.

## First Nations Self-Determination

The strongest examples of community-controlled funding in Australia come from First Nations organisations. Aboriginal Community Controlled Health Organisations (ACCHOs) have operated for over 50 years, demonstrating that communities can design, deliver, and govern their own services more effectively than external providers. The sector employs over 7,000 people and serves more than 400,000 clients annually.

## The Data Gap

Australia currently has no comprehensive database mapping money flows through the social impact sector. Unlike the UK's 360Giving initiative, which tracks philanthropic grants in a standardised open format, Australian grant data is fragmented across ACNC, ORIC, state registries, and individual foundation reports. Building this data infrastructure is a prerequisite for understanding — and shifting — power in the sector.

## What Comes Next

The path forward requires building community-owned infrastructure that generates its own revenue, governs itself democratically, and measures success on community terms rather than funder terms. This means cooperatives, social enterprises, revolving funds, and mutual aid networks — models where the community is both the investor and the beneficiary.
