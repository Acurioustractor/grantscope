## Project Title

**On Country, On Purpose: Aboriginal Enterprise and Cultural Healing in Central Australia**

---

## Project Summary (200 words)

Oonchiumpa will establish an integrated Aboriginal enterprise and cultural retreat hub in Alice Springs and At Napa station, Central Australia. The project creates sustainable employment for Aboriginal young people — many involved with or at risk of entering the justice system — through two production enterprises: goods refurbishment (beds, whitegoods) for redistribution to remote communities, and a creative screen printing workshop producing Aboriginal design merchandise.

These enterprises are combined with on-country cultural retreat programs at the historic At Napa homestead, where Elder-led cultural camps, history programs, and bush skills sessions strengthen cultural identity and connection to country.

The innovation is the integrated model: young people transition from justice system contact through enterprise employment to cultural reconnection in one place-based program. Instead of choosing between "getting a job" and "connecting to culture," young people do both — working in enterprise during the week and connecting to country on weekends and school holidays.

By Year 3, the enterprise aims to generate $260,000+ in annual revenue from goods sales, merchandise, and retreat bookings — creating long-term sustainability beyond the grant period and permanent community infrastructure on Aboriginal land.

## Assessment Criterion 1: Project Impact or Innovation (25%)

### The Problem

The Northern Territory spends **$476 million per year** on youth justice — with **$243 million on detention alone**. NT has the highest rate of youth incarceration in Australia, and Aboriginal young people are massively overrepresented. In Alice Springs, young people cycle through the justice system with few meaningful alternatives. The Central Australian Justice Reinvestment Initiative (rated "Effective" by independent evaluation) has demonstrated that community-led alternatives work — but there are not enough of them.

Meanwhile, remote Aboriginal communities across Central Australia lack access to basic household goods. Families sleep on floors because beds are unavailable or unaffordable. Donated whitegoods sit in warehouses with nobody to refurbish and distribute them.

### The Innovation

**On Country, On Purpose** is the first program in Central Australia to combine circular economy enterprise with on-country cultural healing in one integrated model. No other program in the region does this.

**What makes it new:**

1. **Circular economy enterprise in an Aboriginal remote context.** We take donated and surplus goods — beds from Senegal (100 already received), washing machines from urban areas — and employ Aboriginal young people to refurbish and redistribute them to remote communities that desperately need them. This creates jobs, meets an acute community need, and diverts waste from landfill.

2. **Enterprise + healing integration.** Most youth justice diversion programs offer either employment OR cultural programs. We do both, in one place, with the same young people. A young person referred from court can work in the container workshop Monday to Thursday, then travel to At Napa station for an on-country camp on Friday. The enterprise gives them income, skills, and routine. The on-country experience gives them cultural identity, Elder connection, and healing.

3. **Cultural knowledge driving program design.** The At Napa component is designed entirely by Bloomfield family Elders on their traditional country. At Napa homestead has deep historical significance — it's not a generic "bush camp" but a place of living cultural authority. The museum component preserves and shares family and regional history that would otherwise be lost.

4. **Revenue-generating social enterprise model.** Unlike pure service-delivery programs that end when funding ends, this project creates income-generating assets. Shipping container workshops, refurbishment equipment, and the At Napa retreat facility all become permanent infrastructure that generates revenue indefinitely.

### Impact

**Economic impact:**
- 8-10 new Aboriginal jobs (enterprise workers + program staff)
- Revenue generation: $37K (Year 1) → $140K (Year 2) → $260K (Year 3)
- Skills and certifications for Aboriginal young people (WHS, first aid, machinery, business)
- Supply of affordable essential goods to remote communities (beds, whitegoods, cultural merchandise)

**Social impact:**
- Youth justice diversion — young people in or exiting the justice system get employment + cultural connection instead of reoffending
- Housing amenity improvement in remote communities (beds distributed to families sleeping on floors)
- Reduced social isolation for young people through structured daily activity and cultural connection

**Cultural impact:**
- At Napa homestead preserved and activated as cultural museum and gathering place
- Elder-led on-country programs maintaining intergenerational cultural knowledge transfer
- Young people reconnected to country, language, and cultural practice
- Filming and documentation of young people's stories — their voices, their experiences, in their words

**Infrastructure (permanent community assets):**
- 3 shipping container workshops (goods production, storage, screen printing)
- At Napa homestead restoration — bathroom, deck, paths, camping facilities
- Cultural museum displays and interpretation infrastructure
- Enterprise equipment and tooling

## Assessment Criterion 2: Community Led (25%)

### Aboriginal Families Conceived, Designed, and Lead This Project

**On Country, On Purpose** was not designed by consultants, government agencies, or external program designers. It was conceived by **Kristy Bloomfield and Tanya Turner** — Aboriginal women from Central Australia whose families have lived on this country for generations.

**Kristy Bloomfield** carries forward a legacy of advocacy, leadership, and cultural knowledge from the Bloomfield and Liddle families — pioneering Aboriginal families of Mparntwe (Alice Springs). She has led Oonchiumpa for over 2.5 years, delivering NIAA-funded programs, managing partnerships with universities and the justice system, and building the organisation from the ground up.

**Tanya Turner** is a proud Aboriginal woman from Central Australia with deep experience in youth justice advocacy, legal practice, and community development. Her networks span the NT justice system, community services sector, and Aboriginal community organisations.

### Community Involvement in Design

This project grew directly from community need and community voice:

- **Young people told us what they need.** The young people in Oonchiumpa's existing programs — 21 young people with 90% retention and 95% school re-engagement — have consistently expressed desire for employment opportunities and cultural connection. They designed elements of the on-country program.

- **Elders provide cultural authority.** Aunty Bev, Uncle Terry, Patricia Ann Miller, and other Elders guide the cultural direction of all programs. The At Napa component is entirely Elder-designed — built on traditional knowledge of country, seasonal activities, and cultural teaching methods.

- **Family collaboration.** The project integrates multiple family enterprises and community resources: Henry Bloomfield Contracting (earth-moving and land management), the Bloomfield family's connection to At Napa station, and Tanya Turner's community networks.

- **Partner organisations consulted.** A Curious Tractor Foundation provides supply chain and enterprise expertise. Infuse programs, judges, and school groups have provided input on the justice diversion and cultural education components.

### Evidence of Community Need

- NT has the **highest youth incarceration rate in Australia** — Aboriginal young people are overrepresented at every stage of the justice system
- Alice Springs has **acute need for diversionary programs** — the Central Australian Justice Reinvestment Initiative ($10M over 4 years) was funded precisely because the need is so great
- Remote communities across Central Australia **lack access to basic household goods** — 100 beds already committed to Central Desert communities demonstrates immediate demand
- **Closing the Gap Outcome 11** (youth are not overrepresented in the criminal justice system) is **going backwards** in the NT

## Assessment Criterion 5: Equity (15%)

### Central Australia is Chronically Underfunded

Most NT government and philanthropic funding flows to the **Top End and Darwin region**. Central Australia — home to some of the most disadvantaged Aboriginal communities in the country — consistently receives a smaller share of resources relative to need.

**CivicGraph data confirms this:** Of the NT's $2.69 billion in justice funding (since 2021-22), the overwhelming majority funds Darwin-based programs and NT-wide government expenditure. Community-led alternatives in Central Australia are scarce — the Australian Living Map of Alternatives (ALMA) database records only **3 youth justice interventions** in the Alice Springs / Central Australia region, compared to dozens in urban centres.

### Alice Springs Has Acute Need

- **Highest youth justice contact rates** in the NT — which already has the highest rates in Australia
- **Overcrowded housing** drives young people onto the streets — leading to justice system contact
- **Limited employment options** for Aboriginal young people — especially those without formal qualifications
- **Remote communities** within 150km radius have virtually no access to retail, services, or employment

### First-Time Applicant

Oonchiumpa has never received funding from Aboriginal Investment NT. This is not an established organisation cycling through multiple grant rounds — it is a growing Aboriginal-led enterprise seeking its first major infrastructure investment.

### Reaching Communities with Least Access

- **Goods enterprise** directly serves remote communities — beds and whitegoods delivered to communities 100-500km from Alice Springs where no retail options exist
- **On-country programs** operate at At Napa station — a remote, off-grid property far from any service centre
- **Young people served** are among the most marginalised — justice-system-involved, housing-insecure, disconnected from education and employment

### Closing the Gap Alignment

This project directly addresses **Outcome 11** of the Closing the Gap National Agreement: *"Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander young people are not overrepresented in the criminal justice system."*

This outcome is **going backwards** in the NT. The latest data shows Aboriginal youth incarceration rates continue to rise despite billions in government expenditure. Community-led alternatives like On Country, On Purpose represent the evidence-based pathway to reversing this trend — as demonstrated by the Central Australian Justice Reinvestment Initiative, which has been independently evaluated as "Effective."
