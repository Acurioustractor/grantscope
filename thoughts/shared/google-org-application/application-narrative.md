## 1. IMPACT

### The Problem

Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander children comprise **48% of children in out-of-home care** and over **50% of those in youth justice** in Queensland — despite being 8% of the child population. Successive government inquiries have identified systemic failures in how public money flows to these communities, yet no government department has cross-system visibility into where billions in child protection, justice, disability, and family services funding actually goes, whether it reaches the communities that need it most, or whether funded programs have evidence of effectiveness.

Queensland's *Making Queensland Safer Act 2024* introduced 20-year mandatory minimum sentences, expanded bail laws, and expanded police powers — measures that will disproportionately impact First Nations children. Australia's first Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander Children's Commissioner (OATSICC) has a statutory mandate to hold these systems accountable, but currently lacks the data infrastructure to see across system boundaries.

### The Gap

Governments allocate billions through fragmented systems — procurement, grants, justice funding, community services — with **zero cross-system visibility**. Our analysis reveals:

- **Very Remote Queensland LGAs score 140/140 on our funding desert index** — maximum disadvantage, minimum funding. Aurukun, Napranum, Torres, Carpentaria, and Croydon are all at maximum desert score.
- **$1.88B goes to youth justice detention in Queensland** (54% of total) while only **$1.49B goes to community-based supervision** (43%) — despite evidence that diversion and community-led programs are more effective and less costly.
- **Community-controlled organisations are monitored across 2.15 government systems on average** but receive only **3.2% of federal procurement dollars** — systematically over-monitored and under-funded.
- **487 evidence-based interventions exist in Queensland** (including 37 diversion programs, 65 cultural connection, 65 community-led), but government funding remains concentrated in detention.

### Who Benefits

- **8,619 registered charities** and **1,507 Indigenous corporations** in Queensland, many delivering frontline services
- **4,130 community-controlled organisations** in Queensland who need equitable access to government resources
- Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander children and families across Queensland, especially in the **10 Very Remote LGAs** currently experiencing maximum funding desert conditions
- The Commissioner's office (OATSICC) — gaining unprecedented oversight capability

### Scale of Impact

The Commissioner's AI Toolkit would give OATSICC cross-system visibility into **$3.48B in youth justice spending**, **$1.81B in child protection placement**, and **$2.65B in home and community care** — enabling evidence-based policy recommendations that could redirect resources from detention to community-led alternatives.

## 2. INNOVATIVE USE OF TECHNOLOGY

### Agentic AI Architecture

CivicGraph deploys a fleet of **92 autonomous AI agents** that continuously harvest, deduplicate, and link data across **7 government systems**: federal procurement (770K contracts), justice funding (71K records), charity regulation (66K charities), political donations (312K records), tax transparency, Indigenous corporations, and the Australian Living Map of Alternatives (ALMA — 1,162 evidence-based interventions).

This is not a chatbot or a dashboard wrapper around an LLM. Each agent independently:
- **Discovers** new data sources and records
- **Resolves entities** across systems (the same organization appears in procurement as a supplier, in justice funding as a recipient, in charity regulation as a registered entity, in donations as a donor)
- **Links** funding flows to evidence of effectiveness
- **Detects anomalies** — funding gaps, concentration patterns, equity failures

### Three Novel AI Capabilities

**1. AI Policy Brief Generator**
An agentic system that monitors all data sources, detects emerging patterns (e.g., a sudden drop in diversion funding in a specific LGA, or a spike in out-of-home care placements), and automatically drafts a policy brief for the Commissioner. The brief cross-references:
- Funding flow data (what money goes where)
- ALMA evidence (what programs work in similar contexts)
- Community voice (what families and children are experiencing)

This enables the Commissioner to produce evidence-based policy briefs in **hours instead of months**, directly serving OATSICC Outcome 1.2: *"high-quality policy briefs grounded in data, research and community insights."*

**2. Community Voice Intelligence**
Natural language processing applied to community stories captured through the Empathy Ledger platform. The AI identifies systemic themes, patterns, and gaps between community experience and government allocation. Example output: *"14 stories from Cairns region describe lack of cultural support in out-of-home care. Current funding to cultural connection programs in those LGAs: $0. Three ALMA-evidenced cultural connection programs exist in similar communities."*

Cultural safety protocols ensure AI outputs respect cultural authority, and the CARE principles (Collective benefit, Authority to control, Responsibility, Ethics) govern all data handling.

**3. Diversion Pathway Recommender**
Given a community's profile (demographics, needs, geography, existing services), the AI recommends evidence-based alternatives to detention with outcome evidence and cost comparisons. This directly serves OATSICC Goal 3: *"advance rights-based approaches to youth justice in Queensland (diversion, rehabilitation, restorative justice and community-led solutions)."*

### Alignment with Google's AI Principles

- **Beneficial**: Designed to reduce systemic inequity in public resource allocation
- **Avoids unfair bias**: CARE data sovereignty principles ensure First Nations communities control data about themselves
- **Accountable**: Commissioner has statutory authority and direct ministerial access
- **Safe**: Read-only analytical system — recommends, does not decide

## Summary

CivicGraph proposes to build an AI-powered Commissioner's Toolkit that gives Australia's first Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander Children's Commissioner unprecedented cross-system visibility into how public money flows to First Nations communities — enriched by evidence of what works (ALMA) and amplified by children's own voices (Empathy Ledger).

The system is not hypothetical. CivicGraph already maps 158,724 entities across 7 government systems with 92 AI agents running in production. We've already identified the funding deserts, the detention-over-diversion spending pattern, and the over-monitoring/under-funding of community-controlled organisations.

What we need is the resources to deploy this infrastructure with a government partner who has the mandate and authority to act on what the data reveals. OATSICC — with its direct ministerial access, statutory independence, and urgent youth justice mandate — is that partner.

**The question isn't whether this data should exist. It already does. The question is whether a Children's Commissioner should have the AI tools to see it, understand it, and act on it.**
