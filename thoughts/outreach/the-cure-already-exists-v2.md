# The Cure Already Exists

*By Benjamin Knight*

---

There is a room I want you to sit in.

It is small. The light is fluorescent. The walls close in the way they do in places designed to hold people, not help them. There is no window. You can hear someone crying through the wall, but you cannot reach them.

This is what a child sees when Australia decides they need to be taught a lesson.

We spend $1.3 million a year per child to put them in that room. In Victoria, it is $2.6 million. Across the country, it adds up to more than a billion dollars a year. And 85 percent of the kids who come out go straight back in.

That is the system. Expensive. Broken. And it falls, with devastating precision, on First Nations children who are 21 times more likely to end up inside than their non-Indigenous classmates.

Everyone who works in this space knows it. Every inquiry has said it. Every report confirms it. The question is not whether the approach is broken. The question is what you do about it. Right now. Today. With your hands and your time and your attention.

---

## What Already Works

I want to tell you about three places. Not pilots. Not proposals. Places that exist right now, where the alternative is already running.

**Oonchiumpa** in Alice Springs is 100 percent Aboriginal-led. Founded by Kristy Bloomfield, a Central Arrernte, Eastern Arrernte and Alyawarra woman and Traditional Owner of Mparntwe, and Tanya Turner, an Eastern Arrernte woman who graduated from UWA law school and worked as an Associate at the Supreme Court of Victoria before coming home to bring legal expertise alongside community wisdom.

Together they built something that is not another program designed by outsiders with good intentions. It is leadership rooted in cultural authority, family connection, and the deep understanding that these are their kids, their Country, their responsibility.

The results: 95 percent diversion success. 97.6 percent cheaper than detention. When NT Police ran Operation Luna, a targeted list of 21 high-risk young people, Oonchiumpa worked with them. By December, only one remained on that list. 72 percent of disengaged youth have returned to education through their programs.

Kristy says something that stays with me: *"These aren't 'bad kids', they're children without basics many Australians take for granted. The system responds with detention that costs millions and changes nothing. We respond with cultural connection, practical support, and the authority that comes from being their Elders, their Traditional Owners, their family."*

Oonchiumpa is not a trial. It is being studied by Flinders University and runs ANU's True Justice program, training the next generation of lawyers and magistrates. It works with 32 partner organisations across Central Australia.

On **Palm Island**, PICC (Palm Island Community Company) has 197 staff delivering services across North Queensland with a 78 percent diversion rate. Their philosophy is simple and old: culture is protection. When young people know who they are, where they come from, and who their family is, they make different choices.

In **Mount Druitt**, Western Sydney, Mounty Yarns is youth-led storytelling. Young people writing and publishing their own stories, running their own media, challenging the deficit narratives that follow their suburb everywhere. Thirty percent of participants are now pursuing media careers.

Three communities. Three approaches. Aboriginal-led cultural authority, community-driven family strength, youth-led storytelling. Same truth underneath: when families and communities lead, young people respond.

Last year I visited the **Diagrama Foundation** in Spain. They run youth justice centres. Except they do not call them that, and once you are inside you understand why. Curtains on the windows, not metal grates. Wooden floors. Natural light. Gardens tended by the young people themselves. The staff are called "social educators", not guards. They stay for years, sometimes decades.

Jesus Teruel, the Territorial Director, told me something I have thought about every day since: *"We aren't jailers. We're not just enforcing a sentence to pass the months until they leave. We're here to work with them, to transmit values, to show them there's a different way."*

Their recidivism rate is 13.6 percent. Australia's is above 80. Their cost per young person is around $31,000 a year. Ours is $1.3 million. For every euro they invest, the University of Valencia found a return of 5.64 euros in avoided social cost.

I watched a young woman who had absconded return to the centre. A staff member met her at the door. Gave her a warm embrace. Said, *"Why did you do that? Don't do that again."* That was it. No isolation. No punishment escalation. Just connection.

I watched young women proudly showing me their bedrooms. Teddy bears, family photos, embroidered pillows. These are children in the justice system.

They looked like children.

## The Problem Is Not Evidence. The Problem Is Infrastructure.

Australia does not have a knowledge problem. We have a visibility problem.

Oonchiumpa's 95 percent diversion rate is not hidden. It is published. PICC's 78 percent is documented. Diagrama's 13.6 percent recidivism has been studied for three decades. There are hundreds of programs like these across Australia, run by people who have been doing this work for years without recognition or adequate funding.

But here is what happens in practice. A state government wants to reform its youth justice system. It commissions a Big 4 consulting firm — Deloitte, PwC, EY, KPMG — for $5 to $15 million. The consultants spend twelve months linking datasets, mapping service systems, and producing a report. They look at government administrative data. They do not find Oonchiumpa in the data because Oonchiumpa does not live in the data the consultants are paid to analyse. They find the same things every previous report found. The report is published. Nothing structural changes. The community organisations that already have the answers continue operating on grant cycles, volunteer hours, and midnight phone calls.

Meanwhile, the billion dollars keeps flowing into detention.

This is the gap I set out to close.

## JusticeHub

JusticeHub is open infrastructure for community-led justice reform. It is not a report. It is not a campaign. It is a permanent, public evidence system that makes the case for alternatives undeniable.

Here is what it contains, right now:

**1,155 verified interventions** across every state and territory — diversion programs, cultural connection initiatives, wraparound support, community-led research, prevention programs. Each one structured with evidence type, methodology, outcomes, cultural authority, target cohort, and geography. This is the Australian Living Map of Alternatives (ALMA), the most comprehensive evidence base for what works in youth justice that exists in this country. It is public. It is searchable. It is free.

**$1 billion+ in justice spending**, tracked line by line — which programs, which recipients, which states, which financial years. When a government says it is "investing in prevention," JusticeHub shows you whether the money actually moved.

**143,000 linked entities** — charities, government bodies, Indigenous organisations, social enterprises, foundations, and contractors — resolved to a single identity with ABN, location, sector, and community-controlled status. This is the map of who is doing what, where, and with whose money.

**Funding gap analysis by postcode** — which communities receive the least funding relative to their need. Where the provider markets are thin. Where community-controlled organisations exist but are unfunded. Where no alternatives exist at all.

This is not a dashboard built for a single budget cycle. It is standing infrastructure. The data does not disappear when the consultant's contract ends.

And here is why it matters: **the organisations doing the best work are the least visible in the systems that allocate funding.** JusticeHub inverts that. It starts from the community, structures their evidence, and makes it findable by the people who control the money. It does not require a $1 million evaluation to prove your program works. It requires your program to exist.

## CONTAINED

Evidence changes minds slowly. Experience changes them in thirty minutes.

CONTAINED is a shipping container. One container, three rooms, thirty minutes.

**The first room** shows you what the current system does to a child. Not in theory. Physically. You sit in it.

A young man named Isaiah helped us build the first version. He has been through the system. He knows what the steel benches feel like, because timber could become a weapon. He knows what the mesh windows look like, because glass could become a weapon. He knows the fluorescent light, the bolted furniture, the institutional logic that strips everything portable because hope gets dangerous when it is portable.

Isaiah sat with us while people walked through. He watched their faces. He said: *"I was just watching your face and you're like, 'What the f---?'"*

That recognition is the point. Not sympathy. Recognition. The first room does not explain detention. It makes you sit in it.

At every tour stop, young people from the local community design this room. They are the experts. They know what it feels like. They decide what the public needs to see.

**The second room** shows you what works instead. Diagrama. Oonchiumpa. The evidence, made physical. What a justice system looks like when it is designed to hold people, not contain them.

**The third room** changes at every stop. Because the third room belongs to the community hosting the container. It is filled by the local organisations already doing the work. Not the ones in government reports. The ones running on conviction and community trust. At each stop they fill this room with their story, their people, their proof.

You walk through all three rooms in thirty minutes. Then you step out into the daylight and we ask you one question: *Who else needs to experience this?*

## The Tour

CONTAINED launches April 20 and tours nationally. This is not a conference roadshow. It is a campaign built on geography.

We are taking it to the places where the gap between what we spend and what works is widest. Alice Springs, where Oonchiumpa diverts 95 percent of young people for 97.6 percent less than detention. Townsville and Palm Island, where PICC's 197 staff achieve 78 percent diversion. Mount Druitt, where Mounty Yarns proves young people are not the problem. Darwin, where the NT spends more per capita on youth detention than any other jurisdiction. Brisbane, Melbourne, Perth, Adelaide — the state capitals where the funding decisions are made.

At every stop, JusticeHub provides the data layer. The local funding landscape. The gap analysis. The evidence for what works in that specific community. The container creates the experience. JusticeHub makes it impossible to walk away and claim you did not know.

And at every stop, the community organisations filling that third room are connected — to each other, to funders, to the public, and to a permanent evidence base that outlasts the tour.

This is what I mean by infrastructure. Not a moment. A system.

## Why Beds Matter

Yes, beds.

Goods on Country is led by my co-founder Nicholas Marchesi at A Curious Tractor. We work with remote Indigenous communities to design and manufacture essential goods — starting with a bed made from recycled plastic, galvanised steel, and heavy-duty Australian canvas. It flat-packs. It washes. It assembles in five minutes with no tools. Each one diverts 20 kilograms of plastic from landfill.

We have delivered more than 400 beds across eight communities. We built a containerised production facility — two shipping containers, one that shreds and collects plastic, one that presses sheets and cuts parts. About 30 beds a week.

Why does this belong in a youth justice article?

Because the pipeline is direct and it starts at the ground. Literally the ground.

Ivy on Palm Island told us: *"Hardly anyone around the community has beds. When family comes to visit, people sleep on the floor."*

One in three children in remote communities has scabies at any given time. Scabies leads to Rheumatic Heart Disease. 59 percent of remote homes lack washing machines. Kids who are sick do not go to school. Kids who do not go to school end up in the system. The connection between a bed and a courtroom is not theoretical. It is a line you can draw with your finger.

Fred Campbell, a youth case worker at Oonchiumpa, told us about a young man named Xavier who built a Stretch Bed for the first time: *"He just was so proud showing them that he can build it. After that he felt so happy about himself... his energy was a lot more higher. He's quite capable of building that on his own and sharing that onto other kids."*

Not a courtroom. A young person standing over something they made with their own hands. Proud. Wanting to teach others.

That is one possible alternative.

## What This Adds Up To

Let me be specific about what we are building, because specificity is what separates a vision from a wish.

**JusticeHub** is the evidence system. 1,155 interventions. $1 billion in tracked spending. 143,000 linked entities. Public, permanent, and designed to make community-led alternatives findable and fundable. It launches publicly at [justicehub.org.au](https://justicehub.org.au/) alongside the tour.

**CONTAINED** is the activation layer. A touring physical experience that turns data into recognition, recognition into conversation, conversation into political pressure. It connects communities to each other and to the evidence that proves what they already know.

**Goods on Country** is the material proof that you can build something different. Not just argue for it. Build it. With the community. In the community. Owned by the community.

These three things work together because the problem has three parts: the evidence is invisible (JusticeHub), the public does not feel it (CONTAINED), and the material conditions that feed the pipeline are unaddressed (Goods).

Together they form a new kind of justice reform infrastructure. Not a report that sits on a shelf. Not a campaign that peaks and fades. A permanent system that community organisations own, that the public can access, and that governments cannot ignore.

## The Point

The cure already exists. It lives in communities like Alice Springs and Palm Island and Mount Druitt. It lives in Elders naming a washing machine in language. It lives in Kristy Bloomfield getting a phone call at midnight because a kid needs somewhere to go, and going. It lives in young people learning to build beds from recycled plastic, and being proud.

It is not waiting for permission. It is happening.

The only question is whether the rest of us will catch up.
